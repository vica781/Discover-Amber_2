What amber is the rarest and most expensive


Baltic amber is considered to be of the highest quality, which makes it very valuable. Among gemstones, amber can take one of the first places for its beauty and variety of colours. The palette of shades strikes the imagination of even the most sophisticated jewellery lovers.
Baltic amber is predominantly dominated by yellow, golden-yellow colours, hence the term "amber colour". The colouring of amber ranges from white, yellow, brown to red. There are greenish, bluish, grey and even black amber. There are even subtler shades and combinations among them. Amber can be completely transparent or completely opaque. Amber is not always monochromatic: there are unique combinations of two or more colours and shades, patterns (sometimes they form the most brilliant artistic compositions). This is why amber becomes alluring, charming and unique.

What is the rarest colour of amber?


Blue is the rarest shade of amber and the most valuable. There is less and less blue amber, this colour has almost disappeared. Only 0.2% of all amber is blue, and this colour is usually found in nuggets of white amber. Blue amber was formed when resin floated by rivers to the Sambia Peninsula and got into the soil, which was saturated with pyrite (FeS2). Intrusions of pyrite penetrated the shallow cracks in the resin. This shade is most often found in white amber. Blue Baltic amber is different from blue Dominican amber. The bluish hue in Dominican amber is only visible under special lighting.

Natural red amber is particularly rare and is only found in 0.5% of cases. Red amber can range from orange to dark black. The rare colour is often obtained artificially - by heating transparent amber to oxidise it.
Red amber was formed by exposing amber to air for a long time: the oxidation process, the warmth of the sun, the fire in the forest. Natural oxidation takes place in the air, amber interacts with oxygen and gradually changes its colour. Transparent amber becomes more red; yellow and other colours become more concentrated. This is a very long process: the change of colour shades could be noticed only after 50-70 years. "Old" amber is highly valued because of its "maturity" and has its own temporary patina.
Red amber, called "dragon's blood", featured widely in the Shosoin hoards found in Japan. It was highly prized both for its rarity and its mystical, divine power.

White amber is found in about 1-2% of cases. It can be distinguished by a variety of surface and interior textures and is often referred to as 'royal', 'imperial' or 'bony' amber, some taking white amber for ivory. White amber can include areas of infiltration or patterns of yellow, black, blue, green or clear amber. This amber is usually characterized by a variety of textures and "natural ornamentation". The volatile resin substances evaporated very intensively under the influence of the sun's heat, and the resin takes the form of foam. In one square millimetre of white amber, there can be approximately 1 million micro-bubbles of 0,001-0,0008 mm. in diameter. The more microbubbles in amber, the whiter it is.

Green amber is also rare. It accounts for only about 2%. The greenish hue, and sometimes the "crystalline"/"sugar" structure, was formed after the resin was ingested by plants and reacted with the pigment chlorophyll, which could be found in the plants. Because of its crystalline structure, green amber is sometimes called "sugar amber".

Transparent amber (with a yellowish tint). About 10% of amber is transparent, but they are mostly small pieces. Large transparent amber pieces are especially rare and valuable. This colour of amber can be called the "primary" colour - it is what fresh tree resin looks like. The resin flowed somewhere in the shade; therefore, the terpenes, the volatile components of the resin, evaporated slowly and did not pollute the resin with gas bubbles, so that amber remained transparent. The shade of transparency could vary from yellowish to dark red; this depended on the degree of amber oxidation. If successive streams of resin were stacked in layers, amber was formed layered. It is this amber that contains the most inclusions.

Black amber is a fairly frequent colour, around 15%. The resin was thoroughly mixed with remnants of bark and leaf litter. Sometimes there might only be 10-15% resin, with the rest being impurities. Therefore, black amber is more fragile than other amber colours, and difficult for artists to work with. Black amber often features attractive natural patterns, textures or inclusions.

Yellow is the most common colour, occurring in about 70% of cases, representing various shades of yellow, but this type of amber is often cloudy rather than clear. Resin dripped from trees under the scorching sun, the volatile components of the resin evaporated and made it turbid - thousands of small gas bubbles formed. These bubbles refract light, producing a yellow colour. One square millimetre of yellow amber may contain 2500 gas bubbles of 0,05-0,0025 mm in diameter. The more bubbles, the lighter the shade of yellow.

What amber is the most valuable?

Prices for amber can range from £16,200 to £32,300 or more. Dominican blue amber, the most expensive type of amber. Transparent amber is more valuable than cloudy material. Interesting inclusions of plants or animals increase the value of a piece. The highest values are given to pieces with clearly visible insect inclusions, light colours and transparency.
