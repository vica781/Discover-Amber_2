The human use of amber

How amber became the material for microscope lenses and medical instruments

Soft and viscous, malleable and responsive to the warmth of human hands. Easy to work and polish, retaining its aristocratic glossy lustre for centuries...

This is all about amber, the most amazing and little-studied mineral of the universe. Hand workmanship makes amber pieces unique, and the delicate, scrupulous work of a master craftsman helps to accentuate the natural beauty of the gem. Precious settings give the amber jewellery an utterly captivating look.


Amber boasts a considerable range of useful properties. In addition to its unique decorative features, it has a set of characteristics that allow it to be widely used for a variety of purposes. Therefore, amber is a material not only for jewellery, expensive souvenirs, but also as a raw material for industrial products.

Amber has been faithfully serving science for centuries, and many industries cannot do without it.

From Stradivarius violins to seed "enhancers"

About 800 tonnes of gemstones are mined around the world every year, but only 10-15% of them are suitable for jewellery purposes. What happens to the rest?

In modern industry used contaminated minerals and amber production waste, such as debris, crumbs and flour. In general, anything that is not suitable for the production of jewellery and products, or left over from it. There are many fields of human endeavor in which amber has been used in a different way from what we are accustomed to.

In physics the mineral used to be used to make magnifying glasses, lenses for spectacles and eyepieces for microscopes. Amber-based glassware is better than a traditional glassware. It worked perfectly for the visual approximation of objects and the magnification of their size, but the fragility of the material forced scientists to resort to ordinary glass. And, in museums, you can also find electroscopes and spirit meters made of amber.

In pharmacology since ancient times, the fossil resin has been renowned as a cure-all for many ailments: it was believed that a small piece of it could cure male ailments and heal the soul. The sun stone's high content of iron, zinc, potassium and iodine makes it indispensable in pharmaceuticals for creating medicines. For example, oils which help to quickly treat injuries, sprains, joint pains; relieve muscular pain, treat respiratory disorders, relieve coughing fits.

In medicine amber is used to make utensils, devices and instruments for blood transfusion and preservation. The reason is that the mineral does not absorb moisture and does not let the plasma "clot".

Amber oil is used to create varnish. In art, Stradivari - the famous violinmaker who made musical instruments at the turn of the seventeenth and twentieth centuries, used amber varnish to cover his pieces. Perhaps, that is why Antonio was able to achieve the perfect sound of his instruments, which even hundreds of years later are still being hunted by collectors. And the phenomenon of the violins he made was covered with mystical details.
Amber oil is used to impregnate wood (furniture, ship hulls, buildings and even railway sleepers), thus preventing it from rotting. It is also used in foundries as a moulding oil, and as a flotation oil for enriching ores of various minerals and coal.

Amber acid (succinic acid) is not only useful for people. In agriculture used as an organic stimulant to improve seed quality. And later on, to make the plants grow better, bloom better and produce a good harvest. It is actively used as a natural growth stimulant for plants, birds and animals. Even tiny doses increase yields by 15-40%.

This therapeutic pet collar made from natural Baltic amber is a natural protection for your pet against fleas and ticks. It will also normalise the thyroid gland, reduce inflammation and strengthen the immune system. When amber is heated by the body and rubbed against the fur, it releases terpenes that repel parasites. Friction also produces an electrostatic charge that is unfavourable to fleas and ticks, but completely harmless to the pet.

The same acid is registered as a vitamin E363 food additive. It is recommended for the prevention of nervous breakdowns and adaptation to difficult conditions, so it is often found in the diet of athletes, the sick and the elderly.
Amber acid is used in perfumery and pharmacology, in colour photography, in the manufacture of leatherette, industrial dyes and in agriculture.

Amber rosin (melted amber), constituting two-thirds of all processed amber, is used for production of enamels and varnishes used in industrial production of tins, for covering electric wires, furniture, musical instruments. Amber dyes are of better quality than all other paint products.

In power and instrument-making industry, properties of amber stone are used as insulating material. Pressed amber is used in manufacture of medical ware, instruments and equipment for blood transfusion, due to its properties preventing rapid destruction of human red blood cells.

The mysterious mineral that keeps itself hidden

Amber is also actively used in science and manufacturing, as it is used to make leather substitutes and dyes, and mineral-based compounds are used to insulate wires. The canning, furniture, perfume, construction and electronics industries; foundry, instrumentation, mining - amber being used in so many areas.

The mineral used to be used to make cups, forks, spoons, mugs and plates. Nowadays, no one would think of using such exquisite utensils for dinner parties, but a couple of centuries ago our ancestors were convinced that amber tableware detoxifies poisons. Even religious rites cannot do without the "sun stone": gems of improper quality are burnt during the church services, filling the room with sweet and sacred odour of incense. Shrouded in mystery and myth, an alluring work of nature, about which many legends have been written... Modern technology has made it possible to learn a great deal about amber, but not everything: it remains a great mystery to be apprehended, and hides many secrets.

Amber is now firmly embedded in all walks of life. It is hard to imagine a person who has never enjoyed a fine piece of hardened resin, admired ancient semi-precious treasures in museum showcases, or at least sat at a varnished table. But progress continues - perhaps, soon radiant cabochons will show themselves in a new and unexpected way!
